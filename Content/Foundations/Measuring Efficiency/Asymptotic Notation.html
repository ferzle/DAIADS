Asymptotic Notation

Overview

Asymptotic notation is a language for simplifying work counts. After we count enough operations to understand how an algorithm's work grows, asymptotic notation lets us ignore constants, lower-order terms, and machine-specific details.

The point is not to make analysis vague. The point is to focus on the part of the count that controls performance for large inputs.

Why Simplify?

Suppose one analysis gives \(5n+2\) operations and another gives \(100n+40\). The second algorithm may be slower in practice for many inputs, but both grow linearly. Doubling \(n\) roughly doubles the work in both cases.

Compare that with \(n^2\). Once \(n\) gets large, a quadratic algorithm eventually overtakes any fixed constant multiple of a linear algorithm. This is why growth rate matters.

Detailed expression Asymptotic simplification Reason
\(7\) \(\Theta(1)\) Constant work.
\(5n+2\) \(\Theta(n)\) Constants and lower-order terms do not change the growth rate.
\(\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\) \(\Theta(n^2)\) The leading term is \(\frac{1}{2}n^2\).
\(3n^2 + 10n + 8\) \(\Theta(n^2)\) The quadratic term dominates as \(n\) grows.

Theta: Tight Growth

\(T(n)=\Theta(g(n))\) means that \(T(n)\) grows at the same asymptotic rate as \(g(n)\). It is a tight description of growth.

For example, if a loop always executes \(3n+12\) basic operations, then its running time is \(\Theta(n)\). The constants matter for exact timing, but the growth rate is linear.

Most of the time, when we fully understand an algorithm's running time, \(\Theta\)-notation is the clearest answer.

Big-O: Upper Bounds

\(T(n)=O(g(n))\) means that \(g(n)\) is an asymptotic upper bound on \(T(n)\). Informally, \(T(n)\) grows no faster than \(g(n)\), up to constant factors.

Big-O is useful when we want a guarantee. If an algorithm is \(O(n^2)\), then for sufficiently large \(n\), its work is bounded above by a constant multiple of \(n^2\).

Big-O may be loose. A linear algorithm is also \(O(n^2)\), but saying \(O(n^2)\) hides useful information if the tighter answer is \(\Theta(n)\).

Omega: Lower Bounds

\(T(n)=\Omega(g(n))\) means that \(g(n)\) is an asymptotic lower bound on \(T(n)\). Informally, \(T(n)\) grows at least as fast as \(g(n)\), up to constant factors.

Lower bounds are useful when we want to show that an algorithm or problem cannot be faster than some amount of work. For example, any algorithm that must inspect every element of an unsorted array has a lower bound of \(\Omega(n)\) element inspections.

How the Notations Relate

Notation Meaning Analogy
\(O(g(n))\) Upper bound: grows no faster than \(g(n)\) Like \(\leq\)
\(\Omega(g(n))\) Lower bound: grows at least as fast as \(g(n)\) Like \(\geq\)
\(\Theta(g(n))\) Tight bound: grows at the same rate as \(g(n)\) Like \(=\)

Be careful with the equals sign in statements such as \(T(n)=O(n^2)\). It is conventional notation, but it should be read as "is in" or "is bounded by," not as ordinary algebraic equality.

Why Constants Do Not Change the Class

Consider \(5n+2\). For every \(n \geq 1\),

\[ 5n + 2 \leq 7n. \]

So \(5n+2\) is at most a constant multiple of \(n\) once \(n\) is at least 1. Also, \(5n+2 \geq n\) for \(n \geq 1\), so it is at least a constant multiple of \(n\). Together, those two facts justify \(5n+2=\Theta(n)\).

The same idea explains why \(3n^2+10n+8=\Theta(n^2)\). For large \(n\), the lower-order terms are bounded by constant multiples of the leading term.

Common Mistakes

  • Using Big-O when a tight bound is known: Prefer \(\Theta(n)\) over \(O(n)\) when you know the running time grows linearly.
  • Keeping unnecessary detail: \(\Theta(3n^2+2n+1)\) should usually be simplified to \(\Theta(n^2)\).
  • Confusing faster-growing functions with faster algorithms: A faster-growing running-time function means a slower algorithm.
  • Forgetting the case: An algorithm may have different best-case and worst-case growth rates.
  • Leaving loop variables in the answer: Complexity should be expressed using input-size variables, not temporary indexes such as \(i\) or \(j\).

Reading Comprehension Questions

  1. Why do we use asymptotic notation after counting operations?
  2. What is the difference between \(O(g(n))\) and \(\Theta(g(n))\)?
  3. Why is \(5n+2=\Theta(n)\)?
  4. Why is it misleading to say a quadratic running-time function is "faster" than a linear one?
  1. Answer: It lets us focus on growth rate instead of exact constants, lower-order terms, and machine-specific details.
  2. Answer: \(O(g(n))\) gives an upper bound; \(\Theta(g(n))\) gives a tight growth-rate bound.
  3. Answer: It is bounded above and below by constant multiples of \(n\) for sufficiently large \(n\).
  4. Answer: A running-time function that grows faster means the algorithm takes more work as input size grows.

In-Class Activities

  1. Simplify counts: Convert \(12n+50\), \(4n^2+n\), and \(10\log n+100\) into tight asymptotic notation.
  2. Bound discussion: Give one true but loose Big-O bound for a linear function, then give the tight Theta bound.
  3. Case labels: Find an algorithm whose best case and worst case have different asymptotic bounds. State both.

Homework Problems

  1. Formal-ish justification: Show that \(4n+9=\Theta(n)\) by giving constants for upper and lower bounds.
  2. Dominant term: Simplify \(2n^3+100n^2+5000\) to a tight asymptotic bound and explain why the lower-order terms disappear.
  3. Upper versus tight: Explain why \(n=O(n^2)\) is true but not the best description of linear growth.
