Substitution Method

Overview

The substitution method is the "guess and prove" method for recurrences. First, use examples, iteration, recursion trees, or the Master Theorem to guess a closed form or asymptotic bound. Then prove that the guess satisfies the recurrence.

The proof usually uses induction because a recurrence defines \(T(n)\) using smaller values of the same function.

What Substitution Proves

Substitution is not mainly a discovery tool. It is a verification tool. If you already think a recurrence has a certain solution, substitution lets you check that claim carefully.

  • For exact formulas: Prove \(T(n)=g(n)\) for all relevant \(n\).
  • For upper bounds: Prove \(T(n)\leq c g(n)\) for a suitable constant \(c\) and all sufficiently large \(n\).
  • For lower bounds: Prove \(T(n)\geq c g(n)\) for a suitable positive constant \(c\) and all sufficiently large \(n\).
  • For tight bounds: Prove both an upper and a lower bound, or combine substitution with another argument.

Exact Example

Consider

\[ S(n)=S(n-1)+n,\quad S(1)=1. \]

Expanding a few values gives \(1,3,6,10,\ldots\), suggesting \(S(n)=n(n+1)/2\). Now prove it.

Base case: When \(n=1\),

\[ S(1)=1=\frac{1(1+1)}{2}. \]

Inductive hypothesis: Assume \(S(k)=k(k+1)/2\) for some \(k\geq 1\).

Inductive step:

\[ \begin{aligned} S(k+1) &= S(k)+(k+1)\\ &= \frac{k(k+1)}{2}+(k+1)\\ &= \frac{k(k+1)+2(k+1)}{2}\\ &= \frac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2}. \end{aligned} \]

Therefore \(S(n)=n(n+1)/2\) for all \(n\geq 1\).

Strong Induction Example

Recurrences that call \(T(n/2)\) often fit strong induction naturally because the recursive call may jump to a value much smaller than \(n-1\).

\[ T(n)=T(n/2)+1,\quad T(1)=1. \]

Guess \(T(n)=\log_2 n+1\) for powers of 2.

Base case: \(T(1)=1=\log_2 1+1\).

Inductive hypothesis: Assume the formula holds for every smaller power of 2. In particular, assume \(T(n/2)=\log_2(n/2)+1\).

Inductive step:

\[ \begin{aligned} T(n) &= T(n/2)+1\\ &= \log_2(n/2)+1+1\\ &= \log_2 n-\log_2 2+2\\ &= \log_2 n+1. \end{aligned} \]

The formula is consistent with the recurrence and base case, so \(T(n)=\log_2 n+1\) for powers of 2.

Asymptotic Example

Often we only need an asymptotic bound. For merge sort, suppose

\[ T(n)\leq 2T(n/2)+cn \]

for a positive constant \(c\), and \(T(1)\) is constant. We want to prove \(T(n)=O(n\log n)\). The target statement is: there is a constant \(d\) such that \(T(n)\leq d n\log_2 n\) for all sufficiently large powers of 2.

Assume \(T(n/2)\leq d(n/2)\log_2(n/2)\). Then

\[ \begin{aligned} T(n) &\leq 2T(n/2)+cn\\ &\leq 2\left(d\frac{n}{2}\log_2(n/2)\right)+cn\\ &= dn(\log_2 n-1)+cn\\ &= dn\log_2 n +(c-d)n. \end{aligned} \]

If we choose \(d\geq c\), then \((c-d)n\leq 0\), so \(T(n)\leq dn\log_2 n\). Choosing \(d\) large enough to handle the base case completes the upper-bound proof.

This proves an \(O(n\log n)\) upper bound. A tight \(\Theta(n\log n)\) result also needs a matching lower bound, or another argument such as a recursion tree.

Finding a Good Guess

Substitution cannot prove a guess you do not have. Common ways to get a useful guess include:

  • Compute small values: Useful for exact sequences like \(1,3,7,15,\ldots\).
  • Use iteration: Expand the recurrence until a pattern appears.
  • Use a recursion tree: Estimate the cost per level and total number of levels.
  • Use the Master Theorem: Get a fast asymptotic guess for \(aT(n/b)+f(n)\) recurrences.
  • Strengthen the guess if needed: Sometimes \(T(n)\leq cn\) is too weak to prove directly, but \(T(n)\leq cn-d\) works.

Proof Template

A substitution proof should make the logical structure visible.

  1. State the claim: Say exactly what formula or bound you are proving.
  2. Handle the base case: Check the recurrence's smallest relevant input.
  3. State the inductive hypothesis: For recursive calls on smaller inputs, assume the claim is true for those smaller inputs.
  4. Substitute: Replace recursive terms such as \(T(n/2)\) using the inductive hypothesis.
  5. Simplify: Use algebra to reach the desired expression for \(T(n)\).
  6. Close the proof: Conclude by induction.

Common Mistakes

  • Proving only that the answer looks plausible: A substitution proof must use the recurrence and base case.
  • Using the desired result before it is justified: The inductive hypothesis applies only to smaller inputs.
  • Forgetting constants in Big-O proofs: You may need to choose constants large enough for both the inductive step and the base cases.
  • Trying to prove a false exact formula: Floors, ceilings, or base-case choices may change exact formulas even when the asymptotic bound is right.
  • Stopping at an upper bound and calling it tight: \(O(g(n))\) alone does not prove \(\Theta(g(n))\).

Reading Comprehension Questions

  1. Why is the substitution method often described as "guess and prove"?
  2. Why does the proof for \(T(n)=T(n/2)+1\) use an assumption about \(T(n/2)\)?
  3. In the merge sort upper-bound proof, why do we choose \(d\geq c\)?
  4. Why does proving \(T(n)=O(n\log n)\) not automatically prove \(T(n)=\Theta(n\log n)\)?
  1. Answer: You first guess the formula or bound, then prove it satisfies the recurrence using induction.
  2. Answer: The recurrence defines \(T(n)\) in terms of \(T(n/2)\), so the inductive step needs the claim for that smaller input.
  3. Answer: That makes \((c-d)n\leq 0\), allowing the expression to be bounded by \(dn\log_2 n\).
  4. Answer: Big-O is only an upper bound. A tight bound also requires a matching lower bound.

In-Class Activities

  1. Exact proof: Prove by substitution that \(A(n)=A(n-1)+2\), \(A(1)=2\), has solution \(A(n)=2n\).
  2. Find the flaw: Review a proof that assumes \(T(n)=g(n)\) inside the step for \(T(n)\), and identify why it is circular.
  3. Upper-bound constants: Complete the constant choices needed to prove \(T(n)\leq 2T(n/2)+5n\) is \(O(n\log n)\).

Homework Problems

  1. Closed form: Guess and prove a closed form for \(H(n)=2H(n-1)+1\), \(H(1)=1\).
  2. Logarithmic recurrence: Prove that \(T(n)=T(n/2)+4\), \(T(1)=3\), has solution \(T(n)=4\log_2 n+3\) for powers of 2.
  3. Upper bound: Use substitution to prove \(T(n)=2T(n/2)+n\) is \(O(n\log n)\).
  4. Lower bound: Use substitution to prove \(T(n)=2T(n/2)+n\) is \(\Omega(n\log n)\), assuming a suitable positive base-case constant.
